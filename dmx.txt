* The Case for Simplicity

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
“Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler.”

-- Albert Einstein
#+END_QUOTE

A website should not fight the thing it was built to carry. If the words matter, the design should make them easier to find, easier to read, and easier to trust.

That means I start with the content. Then I ask what the page actually needs. Sometimes it needs beauty. Sometimes it needs structure. Sometimes it needs a publishing system. But it does not need complexity simply because complexity is fashionable.

My preference is for durable websites: static when possible, fast by default, plain enough to understand, and flexible enough to grow only when growth is justified.

* Why I Build This Way

The modern web has become too heavy for its own good.

Too many sites are built as if every project needs a framework, a database, a dashboard, a login system, a marketing funnel, six analytics scripts, and a pile of third-party services just to say something clearly.

Most do not.

For many people, businesses, ministries, writers, and local organizations, the real need is simpler:

- a clear explanation of who they are
- a trustworthy presentation of what they do
- a way for people to contact them
- a place to publish important updates
- a site that loads quickly and remains understandable over time

That does not require technological bloat. It requires judgment.

DMX Digital exists because I believe the web should be useful before it is impressive. A good website should help the visitor understand. It should reduce friction. It should make the important thing obvious. It should not turn a simple message into a software project unless there is a real reason to do so.

* The Working Rule

Complexity must earn its place.

This does not mean everything should be primitive. It does not mean design is unimportant. It does not mean tools are bad.

It means every layer should justify itself.

If a page needs motion, use motion carefully.

If a site needs a publishing workflow, build one.

If a client needs a content management system, give them one that matches the job.

If a business needs ecommerce, scheduling, forms, or automation, integrate those things deliberately.

But do not add machinery just because the industry expects machinery.

A simple static site that communicates clearly is better than a complicated site that confuses the visitor, breaks often, loads slowly, or becomes difficult to maintain.

* Principles

** Content Before Decoration

The page should serve the message.

Design matters, but decoration is not the foundation. Content is.

Before colors, animations, widgets, and layouts, the essential questions are:

- What needs to be said?
- Who needs to understand it?
- What should the visitor do next?
- What information would make them trust the page?
- What can be removed without harming the message?

Once those questions are answered, design has something honest to support.

** Static Before Dynamic

If the site does not need a database, login system, or heavy backend, I do not add one.

Static websites are fast, secure, portable, inexpensive to host, and easy to preserve. They are often the correct answer for:

- local business websites
- ministry websites
- personal sites
- campaign sites
- article archives
- resource libraries
- landing pages
- small publishing projects

Dynamic systems have their place. But they should be chosen because the project needs them, not because they are fashionable.

** Clarity Before Cleverness

The best design is often the one that disappears.

A visitor should not have to decode the page. They should not have to hunt for the phone number, guess what the organization does, or wait for a bloated page to finish loading before they can read.

Cleverness can be useful, but clarity is better.

A simple headline that tells the truth is worth more than a clever slogan that hides the point.

** Durability Before Novelty

Websites should last.

A site should not depend on fragile trends, unnecessary services, or layers of software that nobody understands six months later.

Durability means:

- plain files where possible
- readable source code
- minimal dependencies
- clean structure
- predictable hosting
- content that can be exported, archived, and reused

A website should not become a trap. The owner should be able to understand what they have.

** Workflow Before Features

The best website is not always the one with the most features.

It is the one the owner can actually use.

A publishing workflow should fit the person maintaining the site. For some people, that means a simple content management screen. For others, it means Markdown files, Git, plain text, or a lightweight static site generator.

The workflow serves the work. The work does not serve the workflow.

* What a Good Website Should Do

A good website should answer basic questions quickly.

- Who are you?
- What do you do?
- Where are you?
- Who do you serve?
- Why should someone trust you?
- How can someone contact you?
- What should the visitor do next?

If the site cannot answer those questions, more decoration will not fix it.

* AI-Friendly by Design

The web is changing.

More people will encounter websites through search snippets, social previews, AI summaries, feeds, and machine-readable indexes before they ever see the full page.

That means content structure matters more, not less.

A site should be readable by humans and understandable by machines. That includes:

- clear headings
- useful metadata
- Open Graph tags
- sitemap.xml
- robots.txt
- ai.txt
- llms.txt
- plain text versions where appropriate
- simple, semantic HTML

Style still matters. But machines do not admire your animation. They parse your structure.

If the content matters, make it available in forms that can be read, cited, shared, indexed, and preserved.

* The Plain Text Bias

Plain text is not nostalgia.

Plain text is control.

It is portable, searchable, durable, scriptable, and easy to preserve. It survives changes in software. It can be read almost anywhere. It keeps the work close to the author.

That does not mean every website should look plain. It means the underlying content should remain accessible and understandable without depending on a fragile presentation layer.

The content comes first.

Then the design.

Then the tools.
